Study of the variability of the CD8 T Immune Response

The aim of this intership was to study the variability of the CD8 T immune response. The CD8 T cells are cells that play a key role against pathogenic agent. When these pathogenics agents are dectected, the CD8 T cells (also called Naive cells) will become Effector cells and will be able to fight against the pathogenic agent. The number of Effector cells is dramatically increasing during the immune response. When the threat disappeared, some of this CD8 T cells will become Memory cells which will play a key role in a case of a similar threat.

To study the variability, we had a collection of data among three populations of mice It consists of the number of "actived" cells (effector + memory) along time with the following scheme of relation between the pathogenic agent and the CD8 T cells.

The evolution of each population of cells is described by a non linear system of differential equations.

\[ \begin{array}{rclcrcl} \dfrac{dN}{dt} & =& -(\mu_{N}+\delta_{NE}P)N, & &N(0)&=&N_0, \\ \\ \dfrac{dE}{dt} & = & \delta_{NE}PN+E(\rho_{E}P-\mu_{EE}E-\mu_{EL}L-\delta_{EM}-\delta_{EL}), & &E(0)&=&0,\\ \\ \dfrac{dL}{dt}& = & \delta_{EL}E-L(\mu_{LL}L+\mu_{LE}E+\delta_{LM}),&& L(0)&=&0, \\ \\ \dfrac{dM}{dt}& = & \delta_{LM}L-\mu_{M}M+\delta_{EM}E, && M(0)&=&0, \\ \\ \dfrac{dP}{dt} & = & (\rho_{P}P-\mu_{PE}E-\mu_{PL}L-\mu_{P}^{0})P,& & P(0)&=&1. \\ \end{array} \]

Our aim was then to estimate the value of each parameter of the above system for each mouse of each population. We have used "Mixed Effect Model" and the software Monolix for this purpose. We were able to emphasize the parameters that explain the observed differences in the immune response between each populations. Extended experiments, using the Sensivity Analysis, have shown the key parameters in the immune reponse. These last results can be used to simplify our system of equation.

This work has been conducted in the team Dracula at INRIA Rhône-Alpes and the CIRI team of Jacquelines Marvel at INSERM. More Informations are available in my Master Thesis and on the slides .

MEE: Metric Learning and Classification

When we are dealing with frauds or unbalanced data, we have only few informations or few examples from the minority class (i.e. the fraud), so it is important to take into accont all of the informations we can extract from the examples of the minority class.

The idea of the presented method comes from the Minimum Including Ball problem, i.e. finding the smallest circle and its center such that all the data lie in the ball.

Using the assumption that two frauds can be closed to each other, we have transformed the Minimum Including Ball Problem into a Maximum Excluding Ball Problem. In other words, we are learning, around each examples of the minority class, a ball which excludes the example of the majority class.

We also learn a metric \(\mathbf{M}\) in order to learn ellipsoids rather than balls in the original space. By doing so, we are able to control both size and shape of our ellipsoids. \[ \Vert \mathbf{x}-\mathbf{c} \Vert_{\mathbf{M}}^2=(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{c})^T \mathbf{M} (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{c}).\]

Note the algorithm is close to a Decision Tree algorithm, the difference lies in the learned areas around each positive examples. Furthermore, there is "pruning" phase in the algorithm. We can write the minimization problem as follows: \[ \underset{R,\mathbf{M}}{min} \; \dfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \xi_i + \mu(B-R)^2 + \lambda \Vert \mathbf{M}-\mathbf{I} \Vert_F^2, \] \[s.t. \; \Vert \mathbf{x}_i-\mathbf{c} \Vert_{\mathbf{M}}^2 \ge R-\xi_i, \; \forall i=1,...,n,\] \[\xi_i \ge 0, \; \forall i=1,...,n, \] \[0\le R \le B,\] where \(R\) denotes the radius of the ellipsoid centered at \(\mathbf{c}\), \(\xi_i\) denotes the slack variable and \(\mathbf{M}\) the metric to learn. The problem is also composed of three hyper-parameters: \(B\), \(\mu\) and \(\lambda\) which control both size and shape of the ellipsoid.

We have compared this algorithm on several imbalanced dataset to other machine learning algorithms as Decision Trees combined with sampling methods and SVM algorithms. We have shown that this algorithm performed at least as well as the state of the art algorithms.

Theoretical guarantees about this algorithm have been derived which ensure that, in test phase, the ellipsoid will contain only few examples of the majority class.

Cone: Cost Sensitive Learning and F-Measure Maximization

This work has been done in collaboration with Kevin Bascol, PhD Student in Saint-Etienne.

Based on a article from Parambath et al. entitled Optimizing F_Measures by Cost-Sensitive Classification, we derived an iterative algorithm entitled CONE to optimize the F-Measure using a cost-sensitive approach.

Using the weighting function proposed in Parambath et al. and their main theoretical results, we propose CONE, a new way to assign iteratively the cost of missclassification on each class and a tighter bound on the highest and reachable F-Measure.

Further works on the link between F-Measure and the costs assign to each classes have been conducted to improve our algorithm. We propose a theoretically founded way to prune the space of research of costs in order to increase the speed of convergence of the algorithm.

CONE has been shown to more efficient than a simple grid search method and reach the maximum F-Measure in few iteratinons only. Furthermore, the new derive bound has been shown to be more useful from both practical and theoretical point of view.

Tree-based Cost Sensitive Methods

In order to improve the profits of the retailers, we propose a cost-sensitive approach to classify the payments of the customers (genuire vs fraudulent). In fact, acceting a false check will not lead to the same consequences as refusing a genuine one. Moreover, a good classification will also have a different consequence on the profit if the check is false or not.

Based on the company experiments, we have a conducted a first serie of experiments. We propose three versions of a random forest algorithm which aim to maximize the profit of the retailers. We have studided how to combine the prediction of each decision trees and if it is better to use the predicted label or the probability of being genuine.

Finally, a gradient boosting model has been proposed to maximize the margin of the retailer. The loss function (which translate the profit of the retailers) is rewritten in order to be directly optimized by such algorithm. We show how to rewrite properly such loss functions in order to plug it in the algorithm.

Both methods have been shown to be particularly relevant in the context of imbalanced data. Experiments on a proprietary dataset of bank fraud detection (used by the BLITZ company) in retail transactions show that our cost sensitive algorithms allow to increase the retailer's benefits by 1,43% compared to non cost-sensitive ones (as the one currently used by the company) and that the gradient boosting approach outperforms all its competitors.
